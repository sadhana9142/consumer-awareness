Introduction

In today’s world, we buy a variety of goods and services every day. But do we always get what we pay for? Sometimes, consumers are cheated through unfair prices, poor quality products, or false information. That’s why consumer awareness is important. As per the NCERT Class 10 Economics textbook, consumer awareness means being informed about our rights and responsibilities while buying goods and services. It helps us make smart choices and protects us from exploitation in the marketplace. The Consumer Protection Act (COPRA) of 1986 was passed to protect consumer interests. It provides a legal framework for redressal through consumer courts. Campaigns like “Jago Grahak Jago” also help people become alert and responsible consumers. An aware consumer is the first step toward a fair and honest market.

How Consumers Are Exploited in the Marketplace

  • Underweight and under-measurement
  • Adulterated and defective goods
  • False or misleading advertisements
  • Overcharging beyond MRP
  • Sale of expired goods

Enactment of COPRA (Consumer Protection Act) 1986

The The Consumer Protection Act (COPRA), 1986, was enacted to protect consumers from unfair trade practices and exploitation in the marketplace. It established a system of consumer courts at the district, state, and national levels to address consumer grievances and provided consumers with six key rights. These rights include the right to safety, the right to be informed, the right to choose, the right to be heard, the right to redress, and the right to consumer education. Here's a more detailed explanation:
1. Background and Purpose:
COPRA was enacted to empower consumers and provide them with a legal framework to seek redressal for their grievances against sellers, manufacturers, and service providers. The Act recognized that consumers often face exploitation due to factors like lack of information, unfair trade practices, and unsafe products. It aimed to create a more balanced relationship between consumers and businesses by ensuring accountability and fair practices. The Act also established consumer councils and other authorities to settle consumer disputes and related matters.
2. Key Provisions: Consumer Rights:
COPRA enshrined six key consumer rights:
Right to Safety: Protection against hazardous goods and services.
Right to be Informed: Access to accurate information about products and services.
Right to Choose: Freedom to select from a variety of products and services at competitive prices.
Right to be Heard: Opportunity to voice complaints and have them addressed.
Right to Seek Redressal: Ability to seek compensation for damages caused by defective goods or deficient services.
Right to Consumer Education: Knowledge about consumer rights and responsibilities.
Consumer Dispute Redressal:
The Act established a three-tier quasi-judicial machinery for resolving consumer disputes:
District Forum: For claims up to ₹50 lakh.
State Commission: For claims between ₹50 lakh and ₹2 crore.
National Commission: For claims exceeding ₹2 crore.
Simplified Procedures:
COPRA aimed to simplify the process of filing complaints and seeking redressal, making it accessible to ordinary consumers.
Strict Liability:
The Act imposed strict liability on manufacturers and service providers for defective goods and deficient services.
3. Importance and Impact:
COPRA played a crucial role in raising consumer awareness and empowering them to assert their rights.
It provided a mechanism for consumers to seek compensation for losses incurred due to unfair trade practices.
The Act also encouraged businesses to adopt more ethical and responsible practices.
The consumer movement gained momentum with the enactment of COPRA, leading to greater accountability and fairness in the marketplace.
COPRA was later replaced by the Consumer Protection Act, 2019, which further strengthened consumer protection measures.

Six Rights of Consumers

  1. Right to Safety: Protection from hazardous goods and unsafe services.
  2. Right to Be Informed: Accurate details about the quality, quantity, price, and purity of goods.
  3. Right to Choose: Access to a variety of goods and services at competitive prices.
  4. Right to Be Heard: Ensuring consumer interests are considered by policymakers and regulators.
  5. Right to Seek Redressal: Consumers can demand compensation or repair when exploited.
  6. Right to Consumer Education: Awareness of rights and responsibilities to make informed choices.

Redressal Process of Consumer Forums

*Consumer forums provide a redressal process for consumer complaints through a three-tier system: District, State, and National Commissions. Consumers can file complaints with the relevant commission based on the value of goods or services in dispute. The process involves issuing a legal notice, filing a complaint with supporting documents, paying the required fee, and attending hearings. The forum can order remedies like product replacement, refunds, or compensation. Key aspects of the consumer redressal process:
*Three-tier system:
Consumer Protection Act establishes District, State, and National Commissions.
*Jurisdiction:
Each commission has a monetary limit for handling complaints (e.g., District Forum handles cases up to 20 lakh rupees).
*Filing a complaint:
Issue a legal notice to the service provider or trader.
Prepare a complaint with details of the issue, relief sought, and supporting documents.
Pay the required fee and file the complaint with the appropriate commission.
*Redressal:
The forum can order the company to:
Correct deficiencies in the product.
Replace or repair the product.
Provide a full refund.
Pay compensation for damages or inconvenience.
*Time limits:
There are time limits for filing complaints, typically within 30 days of the lower court's ruling if not satisfied, according to SlideShare.
*Awareness:
Consumer awareness is crucial for effective utilization of these mechanisms.
*Online filing:
Consumers can also file complaints through the National Consumer Helpline website.

Experiments Showing Adulteration in Food Items

  • Milk – Detecting Water, Starch, Detergent, or Urea Adulteration:
    Unscrupulous sellers might add water, starch or detergent to increase milk volume. Common home tests include:
    • Add a spoonful of milk to water — pure milk settles at bottom; adulterated milk disperses.
    • Foam test: Add a drop of nitric acid — starch causes bluish hue; detergent gives froth.

    Watch demo video: Milk Adulteration Test by FSSAI


  • Turmeric (Haldi) – Checking for Lead Chromate or Clay Adulteration:
    People may add rice flour, chalk, or even harmful substances like lead chromate to turmeric. Simple tests include:
    • Add a pinch of turmeric to water — do not stir; sediment and clay pieces settle quickly if adulterated.
    • Add a few drops of lemon juice or vinegar — any fizz or sediment indicates impurity.
